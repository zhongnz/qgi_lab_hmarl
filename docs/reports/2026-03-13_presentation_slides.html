HMARL Maritime Scheduling 01
Context

Why this project matters

Operational context

  • Ports have limited berth capacity and nonzero service time.
  • Vessels trade off speed, fuel burn, emissions, and lateness.
  • Poor local choices create downstream congestion and idle capacity elsewhere.
  • The system is asynchronous and resource-constrained by nature.

Congestion

Too many arrivals to one port can create queueing and delay.

Cost

Faster sailing can improve timing but increases fuel and emissions.

Coordination

Ports and vessels need global guidance, not only local reactive decisions.

Research value

This is a good testbed for hierarchical coordination under coupled objectives.

Project context 02
Question

What we are trying to learn

Umbrella question

Can hierarchical MARL improve maritime scheduling under congestion and resource constraints?

RQ1

How can heterogeneous agents coordinate using shared congestion forecasts?

RQ2

Does proactive coordination improve over independent and reactive baselines?

RQ3

Which forecast horizons and sharing strategies maximize decision quality?

RQ4

How do coordination improvements affect economics such as fuel, delay, and carbon cost?

The project is organized around a chain of questions: coordination mechanism, comparison to baselines, forecast design, and economic impact.
Research framing 03
Environment

Current simulator setup

Topology

5 ports, 8 vessels, and 3 docks per port.

Time scale

1 hour per step and 6 hours default service time.

Travel distances

Synthetic port matrix with routes from 22 to 98 nm, scaled so multiple voyages fit in one rollout.

Fuel behavior

Default fuel is 100; refueling happens only after actual port service completion.

Operational rules

  • Vessels start at ports, not in mid-sea.
  • Travel happens with real physical movement and fuel burn.
  • Schedule deadlines and lateness are tracked explicitly.
  • Messages between agents have latency and different decision cadences.
Environment setup 04
Experiment

How the current baseline is trained and evaluated

Training setup

  • Algorithm: MAPPO with centralized training and decentralized execution.
  • Parameter sharing across vessels and across ports.
  • Current baseline run: 100 iterations, 64-step rollouts.
  • Environment horizon is 69 steps to fully evaluate the rollout.
  • Reported baseline run was trained locally on CPU with seed 42.

Policy setup

Coordinator chooses destination guidance; vessels control speed and slot requests; ports manage acceptance and service.

Optimization

Learning rate decays from 3e-4 to 1e-4; entropy decays from 0.01 to 0.002.

Primary metrics

On-time rate, completed arrivals, vessels served, dock utilization, and total operating cost.

Evaluation

Multi-episode reporting plus step-level traces, action logs, and event logs.

System overview 06
Mechanics

How one simulation step works

Transition kernel

  • Apply due actions and deliver delayed messages.
  • Advance vessel motion, fuel burn, emissions, and arrivals.
  • Update port queue, berth service, service completion, and refueling.
  • Compute rewards and write diagnostics for the step.

Reward formulas

Vessel rV(t) = -(wf Δfuelt + wd Δdelayt + we ΔCO2t + wt Δtransitt + ws Δschedt) + rarr 1[arrivedt] + ron 1[on_timet]
Port rP(t) = racc acceptedt + rsrv servedt - rrej rejectedt - (queuet · dt + widle idle_dockst)
Coordinator rC(t) = racc acceptedt + rsrv servedt + rutil avg_occupiedt - (wf Δfuelt + wq avg_queuet + wi avg_idlet + wd Δdelayt + ws Δschedt + we ΔCO2t + rrej rejectedt)
Δ terms are per-step changes. w terms are weights. r terms are bonuses or penalties.

Progress

What changed since March 6

Corrected vessel behavior

  • No more collapse to constant minimum speed.
  • Arrival commitments now become real schedule deadlines.
  • Vessels start at ports instead of spawning at sea.

Fuel and stalling

  • Fuel exhaustion causes a true mid-route stall.
  • Departures are checked for fuel feasibility.
  • Infeasible routes are capped or blocked before dispatch.

Port service realism

  • Ports track actual vessel IDs through queue and service.
  • Refueling happens after real service completion.
  • Booked and imminent arrivals are now visible to policies.

What is simplified, and what comes next

Known simplifications

  • Port distances are synthetic, not real-world geography.
  • Routes were scaled so multiple voyages fit within one rollout.
  • Default fuel is generous relative to current route distances.
  • This is still a controlled research environment, not an operational map.

Keep transit-rebalanced v3 as the working default because it gives the best current balance of throughput, schedule quality, and cost.

Technical backup: reward notation and interpretation

Notation

  • Δfuel, Δdelay, ΔCO2, Δtransit, and Δsched are per-step increments.
  • accepted, rejected, and served are per-step event counts.
  • avg_queue, avg_idle, and avg_occupied are averages across ports.
  • Optional weather-shaping terms exist but are omitted from the main formulas for readability.

Interpretation

  • Vessel reward drives local operating efficiency and punctuality.
  • Port reward drives berth responsiveness and queue management.
  • Coordinator reward drives system throughput, utilization, and global cost control.
  • The reward remains structured by agent role rather than being one undifferentiated scalar.
